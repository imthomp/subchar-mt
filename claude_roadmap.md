# Extending "subchar-mt": A Research Roadmap for Sub-Character, Morphological, and CJK Representations in Low-Resource MT

## TL;DR
- Your headline finding — morpheme (jieba) segmentation beats sub-character encodings, and sub-char encodings tank zero-shot but partly recover — is *consistent with* but *more cleanly demonstrated than* the published literature; the most publishable pivot is to stop asking "which representation wins on average" and instead explain **when and why** sub-character information helps (rare/unseen characters, semantically transparent characters) versus hurts (it disrupts a pretrained model's learned token-to-meaning mapping), turning a horse-race into a mechanistic, diagnostic study.
- The single biggest open gap your hardware and design can claim: **no published 2024–2026 work applies radical/stroke/glyph decomposition to modern pretrained multilingual MT models (NLLB-200, M2M-100) with reported BLEU/COMET/chrF**, and almost no one has run a controlled, multi-seed, multi-data-scale comparison of representations *as a fine-tuning intervention on a pretrained model* — the existing canon (StrokeNet, Wubi-NMT) is overwhelmingly train-from-scratch. That is your defensible novelty.
- Target an *ACL venue (EMNLP/ACL/NAACL main or Findings, or LREC-COLING for a resource/analysis angle); to be accepted, reviewers will require COMET/chrF (not BLEU alone), a SentencePiece/BPE baseline, statistical significance across your seeds, an unseen-character test set, and a clear linguistic/mechanistic explanation rather than a leaderboard.

## Key Findings

**A. The literature landscape is mature for representation *learning* but thin for representation-as-fine-tuning-intervention.** Sub-character work splits into: radical-based embeddings (Sun et al. 2014; Shi et al. 2015; Yin et al. 2016), stroke-based embeddings (cw2vec, Cao et al. AAAI 2018), glyph/visual models (Glyce, Meng et al. NeurIPS 2019; ChineseBERT, Sun et al. ACL 2021), and sub-character *tokenization* (Si et al. TACL 2023). The consistent finding: sub-character signal helps most for rare/unseen characters and for robustness (homophone typos), but on average it does NOT beat strong word/subword baselines on high-resource tasks, and non-linguistic encodings (random index, byte) often match linguistically-motivated ones — a crucial cautionary result for your radical/cangjie/wubi conditions.

**B. Sub-character decomposition specifically harms unseen-character translation when applied wholesale.** Saunders, Feely & Byrne (WAT 2020, "Inference-only sub-character decomposition improves translation of unseen logographic characters," arXiv 2011.06523) found that "complete sub-character decomposition often harms unseen character translation, and gives inconsistent results generally. We offer a simple alternative based on decomposition before inference for unseen characters only." This directly mirrors your "catastrophic zero-shot degradation" finding and gives you a principled fix to test.

**C. The morpheme-wins result has a clear theoretical basis.** Si et al. (TACL 2023) argue Chinese "does not have morphological inflection... this renders sub-word tokenization less useful since the main advantage of sub-word tokenization comes from... common affixes and root words" — i.e., the *word/morpheme* is the natural meaning-bearing unit in Chinese, while sub-character units are mostly *sub-morphemic*. Psycholinguistics supports a graded view: per Fan et al. (1984), as cited in Tan et al. (Frontiers in Psychology 2021), "Around 65% of compound characters are semantically transparent such that the meaning of the whole character is semantically related to its semantic radical," and semantic radicals are activated during reading — but the other ~35% are opaque, and phonetic radicals carry little semantic signal. This predicts your radical condition should help a *subset* of characters and hurt others, which is exactly the analysis that would elevate the paper.

**D. CJK cross-lingual transfer via shared components is real but fragile.** Saunders et al. showed radical decomposition can help Chinese↔Japanese translation of unseen words; Zhang & Komachi (2019) did Chinese-Japanese unsupervised NMT with sub-character units. But the assumption of free transfer is questionable: Song et al. (2024/2025, arXiv 2411.04822) found Classical Chinese resources gave "minimal impact" on Hanja/Kanbun tasks (differences within ±0.0068 F1, up to +0.84 BLEU), with benefits only in extreme low-resource regimes.

**E. The few-shot/low-resource finding is corroborated.** Edman, Sarti, Toral, van Noord & Bisazza (TACL 2024, vol. 12, pp. 392–410, DOI 10.1162/tacl_a_00651) show "the effectiveness of character-level modeling in translation, particularly in cases where fine-tuning data is limited," and StrokeNet (Wang, Liu & Zhang, EMNLP 2022) shows sub-character gains grow as data shrinks (+5.7 BLEU at 100K vs +2.6 at 1.25M on Zh-En) — strong external support for your "50 examples is enough with the right representation" claim, and a natural set of comparison points.

## Details

### A) Literature landscape: sub-character, sub-word, and morphological representations for Chinese NLP/MT

**Radical-based embeddings (foundational).** The lineage starts with Sun et al. (2014, "Radical-Enhanced Chinese Character Embedding," arXiv 1404.4714) and Shi et al. (2015), Li et al. (2015), Yin et al. (2016) — all exploiting the fact that "characters with the same radical typically have similar semantic meanings." Nguyen et al. (2019/2020) treated characters as recursive structures with treeLSTMs. For MT specifically, "Incorporating Chinese Radicals Into Neural Machine Translation: Deeper Than Character Level" (arXiv 1805.01565) targeted OOV/unseen words.

**Stroke-based representations.** cw2vec (Cao et al., AAAI 2018) introduced stroke n-grams and beat word2vec, GloVe, CWE, JWE, and pixel-based GWE on similarity/analogy/classification/NER, and notably outperformed baselines *more* with less training data (on 20% of Wikipedia). For MT, StrokeNet (Wang, Liu & Zhang, EMNLP 2022, arXiv 2211.12781; aclanthology.org/2022.emnlp-main.434) Latinizes stroke sequences + joint BPE + shared embeddings and reached a then-SOTA 26.5 BLEU on WMT17 Zh-En — "better than any previously reported results without using monolingual data... with an increment of 2.1 BLEU over the scaling Transformer baseline" (24.4 vanilla) — plus 48.1 BLEU on NIST (+2.6), with fewer parameters (59M vs 80M). Crucially for you, StrokeNet's gains were larger in lower-data regimes (+5.7 BLEU at 100K vs +2.6 at 1.25M) — but it is train-from-scratch, not pretrained fine-tuning.

**Glyph/visual models.** Glyce (Meng et al., NeurIPS 2019, arXiv 1901.10125) used historical scripts + a tianzige-CNN + image-classification auxiliary task, setting SOTA on tagging, classification, parsing, SRL (e.g., 80.6 F1 NER on OntoNotes, +1.5 over BERT). ChineseBERT (Sun et al., ACL 2021, arXiv 2106.16038) fused glyph + pinyin + char embeddings, achieving SOTA with less training data. A 2025 ACL Findings paper (Xiang & Liu, "Exploiting Phonetics and Glyph Representation at Radical-level for Classical Chinese Understanding," RPGCM) fuses radical-level phonetics + glyph and notes phono-semantic compounds constitute "over 80% of Chinese characters" (per Chen et al. 1996, ~81%).

**Sub-character tokenization (the closest prior art to your study).** Si et al. (TACL 2023, "Sub-Character Tokenization for Chinese Pretrained Language Models," arXiv 2106.00400) encode each character via glyph (Wubi) or pronunciation (pinyin/zhuyin) then apply BPE/unigram. Two advantages: shorter sequences (up to 40% length reduction → faster training) and homophone-typo robustness. Critically, they found "the differences between these different tokenizers are rather small" downstream and "non-linguistic encoding methods—SubChar-Byte and SubChar-RandomIndex—can also perform competitively" — meaning much of the benefit is structural/efficiency, not semantic. This is a result you MUST engage with and a baseline you should include (code: thunlp/SubCharTokenization).

**Wubi for MT.** Nikolov et al. (2018) and "SubCharacter Chinese-English NMT with Wubi encoding" (arXiv 1911.02737) showed Wubi+BPE gives BLEU comparable to subword models with a far smaller vocabulary (model-compression advantage) and no need for word segmentation.

**Granularity studies.** "Word, Subword or Character? An Empirical Study of Granularity in Chinese-English NMT" (arXiv 1711.04457) found subword best for Zh→En, hybrid word-character best for En→Zh. Classic segmentation-for-MT work (Chang et al. 2008) found that optimizing for a segmentation standard does NOT always improve MT — consistency and granularity matter more (e.g., +0.73 BLEU from MT-tuned granularity).

**Decomposition-depth empirics.** Han, Jones & Smeaton ("An Empirical Study on Chinese Character Decomposition in Multiword Expression-Aware NMT," arXiv 2512.15556; work largely from 2022, building on their NoDaLiDa 2021 paper, arXiv 2104.04497) used CHISE IDS to produce three depths: ideograph-level (rxd1), intermediate (rxd2), and near-stroke (rxd3). On WMT17 Zh→En they found rxd1 ≈ rxd3 ≈ word baseline (~21 BLEU) but rxd2 collapses to 12.86 BLEU because intermediate decomposition "produce[s] smaller-sized characters that can be independent with new meanings that are not directly related to the root character." Since your radical condition uses CHISE IDS, this depth confound is directly relevant.

**Gaps:** (1) No 2024–2026 work applies radical/stroke/glyph decomposition to NLLB-200 or M2M-100 for MT with reported metrics — a genuine void. (2) Almost no controlled study treats representation as a *fine-tuning intervention on a pretrained model* (your exact setup) across data scales and seeds. (3) Little work analyzes *which character types* (transparent vs opaque, semantic- vs phonetic-radical) benefit, despite psycholinguistic readiness.

### B) Related-language directions
- **Japanese kanji / Chinese:** Saunders et al. (WAT 2020) and Zhang & Komachi (2019) show shared ideographs/strokes enable Zh-Ja transfer and unseen-word gains, but wholesale decomposition is inconsistent.
- **Korean hanja / Sino-Korean:** A Korean→Chinese system using Chinese characters as a pivot (converting Sino-Korean words to hanja) improved BLEU by up to 1.5 points — a clean shared-component transfer result.
- **Vietnamese chữ Nôm / Hán-Nôm:** Resources are emerging but OCR-bound: NomNaOCR (ds4v, 2953 pages / 38,318 patches) and fine-tuned PaddleOCRv5 for Sino-Nom (arXiv 2510.04003). Translation is downstream and under-built — an opportunity but data-risky.
- **Classical Chinese:** Strong resources exist — Liu et al. (2019) 1.24M ancient-modern pairs; C2MChn (~615K pairs, Jiang et al.). Song et al. (2024/2025, arXiv 2411.04822) caution that cross-lingual transfer from Classical Chinese to Hanja/Kanbun is minimal except in extreme low-resource settings.
- **Cantonese / dialects:** Active low-resource area — 130K Cantonese-Written Chinese pairs (NLPIR 2023); Mak & Lee (NLPIR 2021); Mandarin-pivot approaches (LoResMT 2024); a Cantonese case study (VarDial 2022) gained up to +11.98 BLEU with parallel sentence mining.
- **CJKV phonology transfer:** CORI (arXiv 2404.12618) and PhoneXL (arXiv 2307.04361) integrate romanized/phonemic transcription for CJKV cross-lingual transfer (NER/POS), releasing the first phonemic-orthographic alignment datasets — directly relevant if you pursue a pinyin/phonology angle.

### C) Theoretical angles for "why morphemes win"
1. **Pretraining mismatch (the strongest explanation for your zero-shot collapse):** opus-mt and NLLB learned a fixed mapping from their native tokens (characters/subwords) to meaning. Radical/cangjie/wubi inputs are *out-of-distribution strings* the encoder has never aligned to meaning, so zero-shot collapses; LoRA fine-tuning re-aligns them but cannot recover the rich pretrained semantics — hence partial recovery, never matching morphemes. This is testable via probing.
2. **Sub-morphemic vs morphemic units:** Chinese lacks inflection (Si et al. 2023), so the morpheme/word is the meaning unit; sub-character units are mostly *below* the meaning level, adding sequence length and ambiguity without adding alignable semantics.
3. **Graded semantic transparency:** ~65% of compound characters are semantically transparent (Fan et al. 1984); semantic radicals prime meaning, phonetic radicals largely don't. So radical decomposition should help transparent characters and hurt opaque ones — a within-condition effect averaging out to "net loss."
4. **Decomposition depth matters:** Han, Jones & Smeaton (arXiv 2512.15556) — intermediate-depth decomposition is markedly worse than ideograph- or stroke-level. CHISE IDS decomposition (your radical condition) is exactly this terrain; document which depth you use.
5. **Inductive-bias / probing literature:** Belinkov et al. (CL 2020, "On the Linguistic Representational Power of NMT") showed character-based reps are better for morphology/rare words while subwords are better for syntax/semantics — a probing framework you can adopt wholesale.

### D) Most promising, publishable pivots (ranked)
1. **"When does sub-character help?" — a diagnostic/mechanistic study (highest novelty-to-risk).** Build an unseen/rare-character test set (à la Saunders 2020), stratify characters by semantic-radical transparency using the Hong Kong Chinese Character Psycholinguistic Norms (Su, Yum & Lau 2022, Behavior Research Methods, DOI 10.3758/s13428-022-01928-y — subjective ratings of AoA, familiarity, imageability, concreteness, and semantic radical transparency for 4,376 characters) and by semantic vs phonetic radical, and show radical decomposition helps transparent/rare characters but hurts opaque/frequent ones. Add **probing classifiers** on encoder states to show *what* each representation encodes (morphology vs semantics). This converts your horse-race into an explanation and engages directly with Si et al.'s "non-linguistic encodings work too" puzzle.
2. **Hybrid / inference-only decomposition (highest immediate payoff).** Test Saunders' "decompose only unseen characters" against your full-decomposition conditions; test multi-view inputs (morpheme + radical as factored/concatenated input). Hypothesis: a morpheme backbone with selective radical backoff beats either alone on rare-character translation while preserving morpheme-level gains.
3. **Representation-as-fine-tuning-intervention on NLLB/M2M-100 (clearest literature gap).** You already use NLLB-distilled-600M; explicitly frame and benchmark radical/stroke/glyph decomposition on a *pretrained multilingual* model — nobody has reported this with metrics. Add the SentencePiece/BPE and SubChar-Wubi/Pinyin baselines.
4. **CJK shared-component transfer under low-resource fine-tuning.** Use shared CJK Unified Ideographs / shared radicals to transfer between Zh and Ja (and possibly Ko via hanja), testing whether sub-character representations specifically *help cross-lingual* transfer even if they hurt monolingual MT — a nuanced angle that reconciles the mixed Song et al. vs Saunders results.
5. **Domain/dialect low-resource MT (resource-paper route).** Apply your representation comparison to Cantonese or Classical→Modern Chinese, where 50–1000 examples is realistic and the "right representation" claim has practical bite.

### E) Publication venues and reviewer expectations
- **Top venues:** EMNLP, ACL, NAACL (main or Findings) for the methods/analysis story; **LREC-COLING** if you frame it as a resource + systematic empirical study (very friendly to representation comparisons and low-resource languages); workshops **WMT**, **LoResMT**, **WAT**, **VarDial** (dialects) as fallback/companion venues. AACL is a reasonable regional option.
- **What reviewers will require (from ACL/ARR review forms and reproducibility checklists):**
  - **Metrics beyond BLEU:** add **COMET** and **chrF/chrF++**; BLEU alone is no longer acceptable and is known to mislead (e.g., copy-bias in Cantonese). Han et al. found BLEU/DA/expert rankings conflict — use multiple metrics + human or LLM-judge evaluation.
  - **Strong, modern baselines:** raw character, SentencePiece/BPE subword, and at least one prior sub-character method (SubChar-Wubi/Pinyin, Si et al. 2023) — not just your six homemade conditions.
  - **Statistical rigor:** report mean ± variance across your seeds (consider expanding from 3 to 5), significance tests, and error bars — your high-variance opus-mt result especially needs this. ARR explicitly asks reviewers whether error bars over random seeds are reported.
  - **Ablations** isolating the source of any gain (e.g., sequence-length confound: SubChar changes length, which alone affects training).
  - **Reproducibility:** released code, exact hyperparameters, compute budget, data splits; a **Limitations** section (mandatory at *ACL).
  - **Linguistic soundness & multilingual consideration:** accurate claims about radicals/morphology; if you touch Ja/Ko/Vi, document the languages/resources properly.

## Recommendations
1. **Pick the diagnostic pivot (D1) as the spine, with D2 (hybrid/inference-only) as the method contribution.** This combination is novel, low-risk, plays to your compute access, and directly explains your existing results. Frame the paper as "Why morphemes win and when sub-character helps: a controlled study of Chinese representations as fine-tuning interventions."
2. **Immediately upgrade evaluation:** re-run existing conditions reporting COMET + chrF + BLEU with seed variance and significance. *Threshold:* if the morpheme advantage holds under COMET/chrF (not just BLEU), the core claim is solid; if it collapses under COMET, pivot the narrative toward metric-sensitivity (still publishable).
3. **Add the missing baselines now:** SentencePiece/BPE and SubChar-Wubi/Pinyin (Si et al. code is public). *Threshold:* if your jieba-morpheme condition does not beat SentencePiece, the "morphemes win" headline weakens — you'd then reframe around segmentation-consistency (Chang et al.).
4. **Construct an unseen/rare-character test set and a transparency-stratified analysis** using the HKCCPN norms (Su, Yum & Lau 2022). *Threshold:* a significant transparency × representation interaction is your key novel result; if absent, fall back to the frequency/unseen-character axis (Saunders 2020), which is robust.
5. **Run probing classifiers** (POS/morphology vs lexical-semantics) on encoder states per representation, following Belinkov et al. (CL 2020). This is the mechanistic evidence reviewers reward and converts correlation into explanation.
6. **Test inference-only/selective decomposition** (Saunders 2020) and a multi-view morpheme+radical input. *Threshold:* if selective radical backoff beats full decomposition on rare-character translation, you have a clean, deployable method contribution.
7. **Stage the CJK extension (D4) as a second experiment, not the foundation** — Zh→Ja with shared radicals is the safest; treat Korean/Vietnamese as stretch goals given the OCR/data risk. Only expand if Zh and Zh-Ja results are strong.
8. **Venue plan:** target EMNLP/ACL/NAACL main if the mechanistic story lands with COMET + probing + significance; default to Findings; submit a companion short paper or resource paper to LREC-COLING/WMT/LoResMT if you build the stratified test set or a dialect dataset.

## Caveats
- **Si et al.'s "non-linguistic encodings work too" finding is a real threat to interpretation.** If random-index or byte encodings match your radical/cangjie/wubi conditions after fine-tuning, the benefit is structural/efficiency, not semantic — you must include these controls or risk a reviewer demanding them.
- **Most prior MT evidence (StrokeNet, Wubi-NMT, granularity studies) is train-from-scratch, not pretrained fine-tuning.** Your numbers are not directly comparable to their BLEU scores; frame comparisons carefully.
- **BLEU at 50–1000 examples is high-variance and can mislead** (copy bias, reference overlap). Your "2× BLEU over baseline at 50 examples" claim needs COMET/chrF corroboration and significance testing before it can anchor a paper.
- **Cross-lingual transfer benefits are inconsistent** (Saunders positive vs Song et al. minimal); don't overclaim — the honest, nuanced framing is itself publishable.
- **The chrF++ gains reported for ByT5 at very low data (Edman et al. 2024)** should be verified against the paper's own tables before citing specific numbers; the verbatim qualitative claim ("particularly in cases where fine-tuning data is limited") is confirmed.
- **Decomposition-depth is a confound in your radical (CHISE IDS) condition:** Han et al. show intermediate-depth decomposition is much worse than ideograph- or stroke-level; document exactly which IDS depth you use, as it may explain catastrophic results.
- **opus-mt's higher peaks with higher variance** likely reflect its smaller size and Zh-En specialization vs NLLB's broad multilingual prior; this interacts with representation choice and should be analyzed, not averaged away.
